Phishing awareness: Hints and tips

Tip 1: Check the Email Address


Phishing email:

There are plenty of issues with this email.
The sender's email address is not genuine and not an education domain.
The email is not personalised and addressed to 'Dear Student'.
The email style must be consistent with the school's usual communications, and the language must also be consistent with the school's usual communications.
The email offers unusual prizes with the offer deemed too good to be true.
The email asks the student to click a link for contact or download an attachment; both can be used maliciously to compromise security.
There is also the urgency placed on the student to act quickly to claim the prize.
The sign-off from the Principal looks okay, but the website address differs from the email domain, which could raise suspicion.

Always look at the sender's email address. If it looks suspicious, it's likely a phishing attempt.

Tip 2: Traceback your behaviour


Phishing email:

The email sender has a suspect address with numbers and 'info-seek'.
No personalisation or even address to a student with only 'Dear User'.
Activity is suggested to have occurred on your account, which has caused it to be locked. Consider your online behaviours and see if this could be genuine.

The link of 'letmein' is a clear sign of intent, whilst most phishing emails are not as easy to spot. Hover your cursor over the link before clicking.

Tip 3: Look for domains


Genuine email:

Whilst there is a 'no reply' which can raise concern, we see this is a system-generated message.
The education domain 'edu.com' is indicated in the email, which suggests the sender comes from a valid education organisation that has gone through the verification process.
There is a link to click; however, alternatively, the student could visit the office to top up printing credit.
Whilst there is no personalisation to the student, an account number may be associated with the student's account.

Check the senders' domain and verification information could not have been accessed by someone else to protect your details.

Tip 4: Be Cautious with Links


Phishing email:

The email sender has 'no reply' suggested you cannot query the overdue book loan.
There is no personalisation with 'Dear Student' and no mention of the book title or how long it was overdue.
There is no capitalisation of 'your' and a threatening intent with the risk of an additional fine, perhaps suggesting you already owe.
The instruction is to directly click the link, which could compromise your personal details or be subject to further malicious activities.

Hover over links without clicking to see where they lead. If the URL looks strange, don't click!

Tip 5: Careful with unknown senders


Phishing email:

Here, we see numerous concerns...

An unknown sender offering money to support future schooling may seem appealing to many people. The email has many spelling, punctuation and grammatical errors.
A significant sum of money is offered based on 'criteria and information off the school'. How did the sender receive such information?
This also offers a different method of sending a 'treasurer' a text message to potentially capture your details.

Be careful of unknown senders and be cautious if you see poor spelling, punctuation and grammar.

Tip 6: Review the email for consistency


Genuine email:

This is more likely a genuine email.
